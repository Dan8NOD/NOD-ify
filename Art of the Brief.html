The Briefing Order

Par 1 — Situation

Every Marine knows the five-paragraph order. S-E-E-C-S. Situation, Execution, Execution, Command/Signal, Service Support. You used it to brief a fire team, plan a convoy, or call in a 9-line. It works because it forces clarity before action.

Negotiation is the same problem. You're entering an environment with limited information, a hostile or uncertain party, and a specific objective. Most civilians walk in without a brief. They wing it. They lose.

You were trained not to do that. This PDF adapts the five-paragraph order to any negotiation — salary, car, contractor, real estate, benefits, anything with a number and a counterparty.

Bottom line: If you can brief a fire team, you can negotiate a deal. The structure is already in your head. This PDF just points it at a different target.

Advice from an NCO I'd follow anywhere:
"You brief the objective before you leave the wire. Same thing here. Know your conditions before you open your mouth." — GySgt. M.
The Briefing Order

Par 2 — Mission

Your mission statement in a five-paragraph order is one sentence. Who, what, when, where, why. No more.

Your negotiation mission statement is the same:

"By December 1, I will secure a salary of $82,000 with a 10% bonus structure in Chicago, because the market rate for my role at my experience level is $78K-$85K, and I have two competing offers."

If you cannot write that sentence, you are not ready to negotiate. You are ready to guess. The difference between a mission statement and a wish is the "because." The "because" is your BATNA — Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement.

Drill: Before every negotiation, write a one-sentence mission statement. Read it out loud. If it sounds like a wish, rewrite it until it sounds like an order.

Example mission statements:
"By Friday at 5pm, I will close this lease at $1,775 with first-month security deposit only, because the landlord has had two vacancies for 60 days and I have two other apartments I can apply to."

"By next Tuesday, I will get a written estimate of $420 for the water heater repair, because I have a competing quote of $480 and the contractor is slow season."
The Briefing Order

Par 3 — Execution

This is the longest section of any five-paragraph order. It breaks into three parts: scheme of maneuver, tasks, coordinating instructions.

Scheme of Maneuver

Your approach. The general shape of the conversation.

Most negotiations follow one of three patterns:

  • Direct approach — Bottom line up front. State your position, state your value, state your ask. Works when you have leverage and the other side respects directness.
  • Recon approach — Questions first. Gather intel before you make a move. Works when you don't know their constraints.
  • Pressure approach — Deadline-driven. Create or reveal time constraints. Works when speed is an advantage on your side.

Pick one before you walk in. Do not switch mid-conversation unless the situation changes. Changing your scheme mid-brief is how fire teams get separated.

Tasks

Specific moves, assigned to specific moments:

1. Open with an accusation audit. Name every bad thing they're thinking before they say it. "You probably think this is too expensive, and I wouldn't blame you." Disarms their argument before it starts.

2. Label their emotion. "It sounds like you're worried about..." If you name it, they can't use it against you.

3. Ask one calibrated question. "How am I supposed to do that?" or "What would need to change for this to work?" Makes them solve your problem.

4. Go silent. After your ask, shut up. Count to five. First person to speak after an offer loses.

5. If they ask for a concession, trade it. "If I can do X, what can you do for me?" Never give. Always swap.

Coordinating Instructions

Timeline. Walkaway point. Communication method.

  • When does this need to close?
  • What's the lowest number you'll accept before walking?
  • Email, phone, or in-person?

Write these down before you start. Decisions made under pressure are worse than decisions made in prep.

Advice from the fleet:
"When the plan hits contact, the plan is gone. But the discipline stays." — SNCO, 2/5
The Briefing Order

Par 4 — Service Support

What do you need before you execute?

  • Data. Market rates. Comparable sales. Competing quotes. Whatever numbers you need to anchor your position.
  • BATNA. Your best alternative. If this deal falls apart, what do you do? If the answer is "nothing," your position is weak. Fix it before you negotiate.
  • Paperwork. Pre-approval letter. Offer sheet. Written estimate. Whatever document makes your position real.
  • An exit. A physical or verbal escape route. "I want to give this proper consideration. Can I get back to you tomorrow?" — stated before the pressure starts.

A Marine who leaves the wire without water, comms, and a casualty plan is a liability. Same principle.

The Briefing Order

Par 5 — Command and Signal

Who is in charge? How do you communicate?

Command: You are the commander of this negotiation. You are not a passenger. If you feel like the other side is driving, pause. "I need a moment to think about what you said." That pause reasserts command.

Signal:

  • Phone: Call, don't text. Tone matters more than words. You lose 38% of your communication in text.
  • Email: Use for documentation, not negotiation. State facts. Ask one question per email.
  • In person: Your bearing matters. Sit up. Make eye contact. Speak slowly. A squared-away presence sets the tone.
Remember: In the Corps, the radioman who doesn't check in is the one who misses the frag order. Signal discipline saves lives. In negotiation, it saves thousands of dollars.
After-Action Report

Battle Drills — 5 Real Scenarios

Drill 1: The Salary Negotiation

Situation: Job offer. They gave a number first. It's lower than you expected.

Mission: By Thursday, secure a $5,000 increase and a guaranteed 6-month review, because you have market data and a competing interview scheduled.

Execution:

  1. Thank them. Ask for 48 hours. "I want to give this proper consideration."
  2. Come back with data. "Based on market rate for this role and my experience, I was thinking $X."
  3. If they push back: "I understand the budget constraint. What's the earliest I'd be eligible for a review if I exceed targets?"
  4. Get the timeline in writing.
Key line: "I'd rather be honest about the number now than start the relationship feeling like I didn't advocate for myself."

Drill 2: The Vehicle Purchase

Situation: You're on the lot. They're running the script.

Mission: By end of day, leave with an out-the-door price that is $2,000 under MSRP, because you have an email quote from a competing dealer.

Execution:

  1. Refuse the "monthly payment" frame. "I'm only interested in the out-the-door price."
  2. Show the competing quote. "I have $X from [other dealer]. I'd rather buy from you. Can you beat it?"
  3. Shut up. Count to five.
  4. When they counter, flinch. "$X?" Eyes widen. Pause. "How do I know that's your best number?"
Key line: "I'm not negotiating monthly payments. I'm negotiating the total price."

Drill 3: The Contractor Quote

Situation: You need work done. First quote came in high.

Mission: By Friday, secure a quote within budget, because you have a second estimate pending and you can bundle two jobs.

Execution:

  1. "Is that the best you can do?" Say nothing else. Wait.
  2. If they don't move: "I need two things done. What's the price if I bundle both?"
  3. "If I pay cash and you start Monday, what does that do to the number?"
Key line: "I have another quote for less. I'd rather work with you. Help me make that happen."

Drill 4: The Real Estate Commission

Situation: Client wants to negotiate your commission down. You've already done the work.

Mission: By end of meeting, secure full commission with a 90-day listing agreement, because your pricing strategy and negotiation expertise justify the rate.

Execution:

  1. Label their position. "It sounds like you're trying to protect every dollar you can. I respect that."
  2. Reframe. "If I save you $15,000 in the sale, does the commission structure matter?"
  3. Trade if needed. "I can meet you halfway on the rate if you give me a 90-day exclusive and handle the staging yourself."
Key line: "You're not paying for my time. You're paying for what happens when the wrong offer comes in."

Drill 5: The VA or Benefits Claim

Situation: You filed. They denied. You need to appeal.

Mission: By 30 days, secure a reconsideration of the initial determination, because the evidence supports the original claim and you have medical documentation.

Execution:

  1. Treat it like a written negotiation. Every piece of paper is a move.
  2. Calibrated question in the letter: "What specific evidence would satisfy the requirement for service connection?"
  3. Provide exactly that. No more. No less.
  4. Follow up with a phone call. "I sent my documentation on X date. Can you confirm it was received and assigned for review?"
Key line (in letter): "I am providing the specific evidence required by the regulation. If this evidence is insufficient, please identify precisely what is missing rather than issuing a general denial."

The AAR Format

Every fire team knows the AAR. Three questions. No rank in the room. Every Marine participates.

Your negotiation AAR (write after every significant deal):

Negotiation AAR Template

1. What happened?
[One paragraph. Objective. No feelings.]

2. What worked?
- [Move that landed]
- [Tactic they responded to]

3. What would I do differently?
- [What I missed]
- [What I'll try next time]

Mission accomplished? [Yes/No/Partial]
Lessons passed to: [Who needs to know this]

Sample AAR:

1. What happened?
Negotiated with a seller's agent on a $380K list. Buyer offered $365K. Seller countered $375K. I asked what the seller's timeline was — they had already moved and wanted 21-day close. Structured the offer with no contingencies and full pre-approval. Closed at $370K.

2. What worked?
- Calibrated question about timeline revealed their real priority.
- Offering clean terms gave me leverage I didn't need price to win.
- Silence after the counter made them fill the gap.

3. What would I do differently?
- I should have asked the timeline question before writing the first offer, not after the counter.
- I left $3K on the table by not probing whether they'd accept $368K.

Mission accomplished? Yes
Lessons passed to: My buyer's agent team
Final Orders

Rules of Engagement

  • Know your BATNA before you start. If you don't know what you'll do if this fails, you're negotiating from weakness.
  • One move at a time. Label first. Then silence. Then calibrated Q. Don't stack. Stacking looks like a script.
  • The first person to speak after an offer loses. Count five. If they ask a question, answer only what they asked. Then stop.
  • Never give a concession without a trade. "If I can do X, what can you do for me?"
  • Disengage when the deal violates your ROE. Bad faith. Unrealistic terms. Personal values violated. The exit is part of the plan.
ROE card (cut and keep in your wallet):

1. Know my BATNA before entry.
2. One move. Then wait.
3. First to speak after an offer loses.
4. Never give. Always swap.
5. Exit is not failure. It's discipline.
