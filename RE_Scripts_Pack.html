How to Use These Scripts

Every script in this pack is based on real conversations from live NOD Academy practice sessions and actual real estate negotiations. They are built on five FBI-tested tools: Mirror, Label, Calibrated Question, Accusation Audit, and Validation.

Three rules:

  1. Never read scripts verbatim. Read them until you own the move. Then improvise from the principle.
  2. The most important line in every script is the silence after it. Marked as [wait]. Do not skip it.
  3. One script per conversation. Don't stack moves. Pick the right one and run it clean.
"Terms close deals. Price just gets you in the room." — Dan Cruz, NOD Academy

Part One · 5 Scripts

Commission Defense

Commission objections are not about money. They are about trust and value. The agent who gets defensive loses. The agent who labels and reframes wins. These five scripts cover the objections you hear on 90% of listing appointments.

Commission · Script 01
The Classic Low-Ball: "Another Agent Will Do It for 1%"
Listing appointment, seller just dropped the discount bomb.
Tools: Label + Calibrated Q
Seller: "The other agent said they'd do it for 1%. Why should I pay you more?"
You: "It sounds like you've been burned on fees before — or you're not sure the difference in commission translates to anything real for you." [wait]
Seller: "Honestly, I just don't see why agents make so much on one deal."
You: "That's fair. Let me ask you this — what would it cost you if the house sat on the market 30 extra days because the pricing or negotiation was off?"
Seller: "Well... a lot, I guess."
You: "The last three homes I listed at full commission averaged 8 days on market and sold at 101% of list. The gap between good negotiation and average negotiation isn't the commission — it's the close price. Does that math work for you?"
💡 Frame commission as insurance against a bad outcome, not a cost. Label first, then the data. They're buying certainty, not a service.
Commission · Script 02
"Can You Come Down?" — The Soft Ask
The seller likes you but wants a discount out of habit.
Tools: Accusation Audit + Swap
Seller: "I love everything you said. I just feel like the commission is a lot. Is there any flexibility?"
You: "I want to be straight with you about something before I answer. I know I'm asking for more than some of my competitors. And I know this might feel like I'm not being flexible. I hear that."
You: "Here's what I can do: if you can give me a 90-day exclusive listing agreement instead of 60, I'm willing to absorb the staging consultation — that's a $400 value. Is that the kind of flexibility that would make this easier?"
💡 Never just cut the rate — swap it. You extend your runway, they feel like they won. Both sides gain.
Commission · Script 03
"We're Going With a Flat Fee Broker"
Seller has essentially decided on a discount service. One last shot.
Tools: Label + Story
Seller: "We've decided to go with a flat-fee service. It's just $500 to list."
You: "That makes complete sense. You're trying to protect your net. I would too." [wait]
You: "Can I ask you one thing before you finalize? I had a seller last spring who did exactly that — flat fee, saved $12,000 on commission. The house got two lowball offers, both fell through on inspection because no one knew how to structure contingency language. It sat 94 days and sold for $18,000 under the first offer she'd turned down."
You: "I'm not saying that's your outcome. I just want you to have that picture before you decide. What would 94 days on the market cost you in carrying costs?"
💡 You're not arguing — you're planting a story. The calibrated Q at the end makes them calculate their own risk.
Commission · Script 04
"My Brother-in-Law Is an Agent and He'll Do It Free"
The family obligation objection. Tricky — don't attack the relationship.
Tools: Validation + Calibrated Q
Seller: "My brother-in-law just got his license. He said he'd do it for free as a favor."
You: "That makes total sense, and honestly that's a real gift. Family wanting to help is something you don't take for granted." [wait]
You: "I'm just curious — what happens to the dinner table if the deal gets complicated? An inspection issue, a buyer who wants to renegotiate after appraisal, a closing that almost falls apart?"
Seller: "...I hadn't thought about that."
You: "It's worth thinking about. The best gift your brother-in-law can give you might be referring you to someone who's done 200 of these — and then celebrating when it closes clean."
💡 Never attack the brother-in-law. Attack the situation. The family relationship is sacred — make them see you're protecting it.
Commission · Script 05
The Post-Listing Commission Cut Request
House has been sitting. Seller wants to cut commission to attract buyers.
Tools: Label + Reframe + Calibrated Q
Seller: "We've been on market 45 days. I want to cut the buyer's agent commission to 1% to save money."
You: "It sounds like you're feeling the pressure of time — and you want to control something you can control." [wait]
You: "Here's what I'm seeing in the data. Homes that cut buyer's agent commission get shown less — not more. Agents steer clients to homes where they're fully compensated. Cutting from 2.5% to 1% in this market likely reduces showings by 30-40%."
You: "What if instead we looked at a $5,000 price reduction? That actually puts money in the buyer's pocket — and it shows up on the search results as a new price drop. Which do you think gets more showings?"
💡 The seller wants control. Give it to them on the right lever — price, not commission. Reframe the math and let them choose.
Part Two · 5 Scripts

Listing Presentation

You are not the only agent they're interviewing. These five scripts help you stand apart without competing on price — by leading with their pain, their fears, and their goals before you say a word about yourself.

Listing · Script 06
Opening the Appointment: Lead with Their Pain
First 60 seconds. Three sellers interviewing you. Most agents start with their track record.
Tools: Accusation Audit + Label + Calibrated Q
You: "Before I tell you anything about me — I want to ask you something. You've probably heard a lot of agents today talk about their average days on market and their list-to-sale ratio. And honestly, that starts to blur together."
You: "So let me ask instead: what's keeping you up at night about this move?" [wait]
Seller: "Honestly, we're worried about pricing right. We don't want to leave money on the table."
You: "It sounds like getting the number right matters more to you than speed." [wait]
Seller: "Yes, exactly."
You: "Good. Then let me show you how I approach pricing — because that's where most sellers leave $10,000-$30,000 on the table without knowing it."
💡 You just found their priority in 60 seconds without PowerPoint. Now everything you present is in service of that priority.
Listing · Script 07
"We Need to Think About It" — End of Appointment
They liked you. They're not saying no. They're stalling.
Tools: Label + No-Oriented Question
Seller: "We want to think it over and get back to you."
You: "Of course. And it sounds like there's something that isn't fully settled for you yet — and I'd rather you have clarity than rush." [wait]
Seller: "It's just a big decision."
You: "Is there anything I said today — or didn't say — that's creating a question for you? I'd rather answer it now than have you sitting with it this weekend."
Seller: "Actually... we're comparing your marketing plan with the other agent's."
You: "Perfect. Can you tell me what their plan looks like? I want to make sure you're comparing the same things."
💡 "Think it over" usually means "I have a question I don't want to say." No-oriented framing ("is there anything I didn't say") releases the real blocker.
Listing · Script 08
Overpriced Listing: Seller Wants More Than Market
Your CMA says $380K. Seller says they need $420K. This is the hardest conversation in real estate.
Tools: Validation + Reframe + Calibrated Q
Seller: "We put $40,000 into this kitchen. We need $420,000."
You: "That makes complete sense. You invested real money and real time into this home, and you need to see it reflected in the price. I would feel exactly the same way." [wait]
You: "Here's what I'm looking at in the data. The three homes that sold in your zip code in the last 90 days — buyers didn't pay for the renovation cost. They paid for what those renovations did to the comparable value. The market puts your kitchen at $8,000-$12,000 in added value."
You: "If we list at $420K, here's what I'm concerned about: the first 21 days are your highest traffic. If the price creates hesitation, we lose those buyers. They don't come back. A price drop later gets less attention than a right price on day one."
You: "Would you be open to listing at $389K for 30 days, with a clear plan to evaluate? If I'm wrong, we adjust. If I'm right, we get you multiple offers and you may net more than $420K — without the carrying cost."
💡 Validate the investment. Don't dispute feelings. Then data. Then a limited commitment — "30 days with a plan" is easier to say yes to than "trust me forever."
Listing · Script 09
Expired Listing: One Shot at a Second Chance
Calling an expired listing. The last agent burned them.
Tools: Accusation Audit + Label
You: "I want to call out something before I pitch you anything. You just spent months on the market with no result. The last thing you probably want is another agent telling you it was the pricing or the marketing — like you should have done something differently."
Seller: "...Yeah, that's exactly it."
You: "It sounds like the frustration isn't just about the house — it's about the process. And about not knowing why." [wait]
Seller: "We were never told why we weren't getting offers."
You: "That's the gap I want to close for you today — before you decide anything. Can I spend 15 minutes showing you exactly what happened in the market during your listing period, and what's changed since?"
💡 Your opening move is empathy for the pain, not a pitch. You just became the only agent who didn't immediately defend the industry.
Listing · Script 10
Competing Against a Friend or Referral Agent
You're the unknown. They have a "friend in the business."
Tools: Label + Calibrated Q
Seller: "We feel kind of obligated to go with our neighbor — she's been in real estate for years."
You: "That makes total sense. Loyalty and relationship matter — those are the right things to factor in." [wait]
You: "I'll just ask one thing: is your primary goal to make your neighbor feel good about getting your listing — or to maximize what you walk away with at closing?"
Seller: "When you put it that way..."
You: "I'm not saying don't use her. I'm saying you owe yourself one honest comparison. Give me 20 minutes to show you my numbers. If hers are better, I'll tell you to hire her."
💡 You're not attacking the friends. You're separating the emotional decision from the financial one — and volunteering to lose if they're better. That confidence is disarming.
Part Three · 5 Scripts

Offer Battles

Whether you're representing a buyer who's losing to competition or a seller with five offers, these scripts win with framing — not just price.

Offer · Script 11
Calling the Listing Agent Before Submitting a Competitive Offer
You want to know what the seller actually needs before you write the offer.
Tools: Calibrated Q + Label
You: "Before I write this offer, I want to make sure I'm not missing something. What matters most to your seller beyond price?"
LA: "Honestly, they've already moved. They want to close fast — ideally 21 days."
You: "And it sounds like timing certainty matters more than chasing the highest number?" [wait]
LA: "I mean... yes. A clean, certain deal."
You: "Got it. I'm going to structure this with a 21-day close, pre-approval from a direct lender, and a limited inspection window with a repair cap. If I do that — what does the price conversation look like?"
💡 This call is worth $10,000-$30,000. Most agents skip it. The listing agent just told you how to win without being the highest offer.
Offer · Script 12
Multiple Offer Situation: How to Present Your Buyer's Offer
You're calling to present your buyer's offer. There are 4 others.
Tools: Framing + Calibrated Q
You: "Before I send the formal docs, I want to walk you through why I think this offer is the strongest — not just on price."
You: "My buyer is pre-approved with [lender name] — not pre-qualified, approved. We've structured a 21-day close. We're limiting inspection to major structural only and capping repair requests at $3,000. We have no contingency on their current home."
You: "Here's my question: if a cleaner offer came in $5,000 lower but the certainty of close was 40% higher — how would your seller weigh that?"
💡 You're reframing price into probability of closing. Sellers who've had deals fall apart respond to this hard.
Offer · Script 13
Buyer's Offer Gets Countered High: Don't Just Agree
Seller counters your offer by $15,000. Your buyer is at their ceiling.
Tools: Flinch + Calibrated Q + Swap
LA: "The seller countered at $395,000."
You: "$395,000?" [pause 3 seconds]
You: "That's a significant jump. What did the seller say was driving that number?"
LA: "They have another offer coming in this week."
You: "If that offer doesn't materialize — is there a backup plan?" [wait]
LA: "...I mean, they'd want to keep negotiating."
You: "My buyer can move to $387,500 with a pre-approval letter and an immediate 5-day close. But I need the seller to acknowledge that the terms make this offer worth more than a higher number with contingencies. Can we get there?"
💡 Flinch first, test the deadline ("backup plan?"), then swap price for terms. Never just accept the counter — always probe first.
Offer · Script 14
As the Listing Agent: Getting Buyers to Sharpen Their Offers
You have 3 offers. You want to run a best-and-final process.
Tools: Anchor + Deadline
You (to each BA): "We have a strong offer in hand with solid terms. It expires at 5pm Friday. I'm calling everyone with an active offer to give them the opportunity to come back with their best and final."
You: "I can tell you that price alone won't determine this decision — my seller cares about certainty of close. What can you do before 5pm?"
💡 You've anchored that there's competition, created a real deadline, and signaled that terms matter. All three levers in one call. Now they sharpen themselves.
Offer · Script 15
Lowball Offer Comes In: How to Respond Without Insulting the Buyer
Buyer offers $40K under ask. Your seller is offended.
Tools: Label + Calibrated Q
You (to seller): "It sounds like this offer felt insulting — and I get that. They're treating your home like it's a distressed property." [wait]
Seller: "Exactly. We don't even want to respond."
You: "Here's the thing — your counter is a communication. If you don't respond, they walk away and you never know if they had flexibility. If you counter at full list with a tight deadline, you find out exactly who you're dealing with. Want to send a message or find out if there's a deal?"
💡 The seller wants to feel respected. Validate that first. Then reframe the counter as intel-gathering, not concession. They're more likely to respond strategically than emotionally.
Part Four · 5 Scripts

Price Reductions

The price reduction conversation is the most avoided conversation in real estate. These scripts make it easier — by framing it as the seller's decision, not your recommendation.

Price · Script 16
30 Days, No Offers: Bringing the Data
House has been sitting. Time to have the conversation.
Tools: Label + Data + Calibrated Q
You: "I want to share something with you and I want to do it straight. We've had 22 showings in 30 days. Good traffic. No offers. That tells me the product is right — people want to see the house. Something in the presentation is creating hesitation once they're inside."
You: "The most common feedback in my showing reports is price against comparable homes. Three homes in your range sold in 19 days this month. They were priced $12,000-$18,000 less than your list."
You: "I'm not saying you have to reduce. What I am saying is: staying at this price for another 30 days is a decision. It costs you $2,400 in mortgage payments. In your view, what would tell you it's time to adjust?"
💡 You're not recommending. You're giving them the decision. "What would tell you it's time?" makes them set their own trigger — and own it.
Price · Script 17
Seller Refuses to Reduce: Holding Firm
You've recommended twice. They still won't move.
Tools: Validation + Honest Frame
Seller: "We're not reducing. We'll wait for the right buyer."
You: "That makes sense — and it's absolutely your call. I want to be your partner here, not push you. The home is priced where you feel it belongs, and I respect that."
You: "I do want to make sure you have the full picture so this is your decision with open eyes. At the current price, our market absorption rate suggests we'd need to wait 90-120 more days based on who's actively buying in this range. If rates move up, that window shrinks. I'll keep marketing hard — I just want that context clear between us. Does that work?"
💡 Validate first, then plant the timeline seed without ultimatums. They'll think about that 90-120 days number every week. They'll call you when they're ready.
Price · Script 18
Appraisal Came in Low: Renegotiating After Appraisal
Home appraised $20,000 under contract price. Deal is in jeopardy.
Tools: Reframe + Multiple Options
You (to your seller): "Here's the situation straight. The appraisal came in at $350K. We're contracted at $370K. The buyer's lender won't fund the gap."
You: "We have three paths. One: seller reduces to $350K. Two: buyer brings the $20K gap in cash — we can ask. Three: we split it — seller drops $10K, buyer covers $10K cash. Which of those can you live with?"
Seller: "I'm not going all the way to $350K."
You: "Then let's go to the buyer with Option 3 first. If they say no, we have a real decision to make — but let's not leave $10K on the table by assuming the answer."
💡 Multiple options = better than one demand. Option 3 is your real target — present it as the middle-ground compromise between Options 1 and 2.
Price · Script 19
Inspection Renegotiation: Buyer Comes Back With a List
After inspection, buyer submits 12 repair requests totaling $22,000.
Tools: Calibrated Q + Swap
You (to BA): "$22,000 in repairs — that's a significant ask. Can you walk me through which of these your buyer considers deal-breakers versus preferences?"
BA: "Really it's the roof and the HVAC. The rest they're flexible on."
You: "So the deal question is really $14,000 — not $22,000. That's a different conversation. My seller isn't in a position to do repairs, but I can ask about a credit at closing. If we offer a $9,500 credit on those two items, does your buyer stay in the deal?"
💡 Always make them separate deal-breakers from preferences. The real number is almost always smaller than the list total. Then credit instead of repair — faster, cleaner, seller keeps control.
Price · Script 20
Buyer's Remorse: "We Want to Renegotiate or Walk"
Two days before closing, buyer gets cold feet and calls with demands.
Tools: Label + Deadline Dynamics
BA: "My buyer is having second thoughts. They want a $10,000 price reduction or they're walking."
You: "It sounds like your buyer is feeling the weight of the decision — closing is a real moment and it's normal for the nerves to hit. I get it." [wait]
You: "Here's my honest read: we're two days from close. Your buyer has a signed contract. The demand has no inspection basis. If they walk now, they're in breach — and they lose their earnest money and potentially face a suit."
You: "I'm going to ask you to go back to them with this: the seller is not reducing. They are asking your buyer to close as agreed — and they're offering to leave the refrigerator and the garage shelving, which they were planning to take. That's about $800 in real value. Is there a version of that conversation where your buyer gets to feel like they won something and this closes Thursday?"
💡 Name the legal reality without being threatening. Then give the buyer a small win — the fridge / shelving — so they can feel like they extracted something. Everyone needs to save face.
Part Five · 5 Scripts

Buyer Objections

These scripts handle the five buyer conversations that stall or kill deals — from rate anxiety to commitment hesitation.

Buyer · Script 21
"The Rates Are Too High — We're Going to Wait"
Buyers want to wait for rates to drop. They may wait forever.
Tools: Validation + Reframe + Calibrated Q
Buyer: "We're just going to wait until rates come down to 5%."
You: "That makes sense — and rates absolutely matter to your monthly payment. I'm not going to argue that." [wait]
You: "Here's the question I'd sit with: when rates drop to 5%, what happens to prices? In this market, the last time rates moved down 1%, inventory tightened and prices went up 7% in 4 months. You may save $200 a month on the payment — and pay $30,000 more for the house."
You: "Would you feel better knowing your monthly payment exactly, compared to what waiting would actually cost you in the real scenario — not the theoretical one?"
💡 Don't argue rates. Reframe the math. "Date the rate, marry the house" — but show it with real numbers, not a slogan.
Buyer · Script 22
"We Want to See More Houses Before We Decide"
You've shown 15 homes. They've loved three. Fear of commitment.
Tools: Label + Calibrated Q
Buyer: "We just want to see what else is out there before we make a decision."
You: "It sounds like you're not sure what 'right' looks like yet — or you're worried you'll find it right after you commit." [wait]
Buyer: "Kind of both, honestly."
You: "If the perfect house existed in your price range — described exactly the way you described it to me on day one — and you walked in tomorrow, would you make an offer?"
Buyer: "Yes, absolutely."
You: "Then tell me what that house looks like in three words. Because I think we've seen it — and I want to make sure we're not overthinking this."
💡 The "three words" question sharpens their criteria and anchors the conversation on decision, not more searching. You're not pushing — you're clarifying.
Buyer · Script 23
"We Don't Want to Use a Buyer's Agent — We'll Go Direct"
Post-NAR settlement world. Buyer wants to go direct and save the commission.
Tools: Accusation Audit + Calibrated Q
Buyer: "We've been reading online — we can just deal directly with the listing agent and save the buyer's agent fee."
You: "You're right that you can do that — and I won't pretend transaction-only models don't exist. I want to tell you what you're actually trading before you decide." [wait]
You: "The listing agent represents the seller. Their legal duty is to the seller's best outcome. When you negotiate inspection credits, appraisal gaps, or closing terms — who is working for your outcome?"
You: "I'm not the right buyer's agent for everyone. But what I can do for you — in terms of offer structure, contract language, and knowing when to walk — is worth multiples of my fee. What would it cost you to lose this deal on a technicality in the contract?"
💡 Don't get defensive about your fee. Make them think about the specific risk they're accepting. The inspection and appraisal scenarios land every time.
Buyer · Script 24
Buyer Wants to Lowball Before Seeing Comps
Buyer says "let's just go in really low and see what happens."
Tools: Honest Frame + Story
Buyer: "Let's just offer $50,000 under and see what happens."
You: "I'll write whatever you want me to write — it's your money and your decision. I want to give you one picture first." [wait]
You: "This home has been on market 11 days. In this neighborhood, homes in this range have sold in an average of 18 days at 99% of list price. A $50K-under offer signals to this seller one of two things: you don't know the market, or you're not serious. Either way, they counter high or they don't counter at all."
You: "What's your real ceiling? Let's build an offer that's your best position — not your opening position. Those are two different numbers."
💡 You're protecting them from a losing strategy, not their budget. "Opening vs ceiling" reframes the conversation from negotiation theater to real strategy.
Buyer · Script 25
After an Accepted Offer: Managing Buyer Cold Feet
Offer accepted. Buyer texts you at 11pm: "Did we do the right thing?"
Tools: Label + Validation + Story
Buyer: "I'm second-guessing everything. What if we overpaid? What if we find something better next month?"
You: "This is completely normal — and it actually tells me you're taking this seriously, which means you'll make good decisions as homeowners." [wait]
You: "Here's what I know: you didn't make an emotional decision. We ran the comps. You came in at $2K over a supported comp price. You have a 10-day inspection window where you can walk with full earnest money back if anything surfaces."
You: "The question 'what if something better comes up' is real — but I've watched clients wait for 'better' for 18 months in this market. You found something that met your criteria. That's worth trusting."
💡 Validate the anxiety. Then replace it with data they already agreed to. The inspection reminder is your ace — it's their real safety net and most forget it they have it.

The 5 Tools Behind All 25 Scripts

Mirror           Repeat last 1-3 words, rising tone. Then wait.
Label            "It sounds like..." + name the emotion. Then wait.
Calibrated Q     "How..." or "What..." to make them solve your problem.
Accusation Audit Name every bad thing they're thinking. First. Out loud.
Validation       "That makes sense because..." + "And..." (never "but").
Pro Tip from 1,000+ Sessions: The [wait] marker in every script is not optional. The silence after a label or calibrated question is where the other side reveals their real position. Most rookies break the silence within 2 seconds. Two seconds of silence feels like two minutes. Count five internally. Whatever they say next is the most important information in the conversation.
